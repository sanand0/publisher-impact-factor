The Publisher With Five Excellent Journals and 900 Papers Hiding Them

Wiley publishes more neuroscience papers than any other publisher in this dataset. Nearly four times as many as Springer Nature. This should be a strength. Instead, it is the single biggest constraint on Wiley's citation performance — and the reason a portfolio with Annals of Neurology and Epilepsia inside it averages only 17 citations per work.

Consider a thought experiment. Take five Wiley journals — Annals of Neurology, British Journal of Pharmacology, Epilepsia, Angewandte Chemie International Edition, and the Journal of Comparative Neurology. Isolate them. Calculate their average citation rate. The result: approximately 48 citations per work. That hypothetical publisher would rank between PLOS and Springer Nature — the second or third most-cited neuroscience publisher in this dataset.

Now add the other 880 papers. Watch the average fall from 48 to 17.

This is the Wiley paradox. It is the publisher with the most papers and the least apparent impact — despite containing some of the best-performing journals in the entire dataset. The question is not whether Wiley can improve. It is how to stop an excellent core from being statistically invisible inside its own portfolio.

The Volume Paradox

85% of the Dementia Market. 9.2 Citations Per Paper.

The most striking number in the Wiley dataset is its market share in Dementia and Cognitive Impairment Research: 110 of 130 works across all five publishers — a commanding 85% share. Wiley essentially owns this topic. And the topic averages 9.2 citations per work across the market.

That is not a terrible citation rate in isolation. But when 110 papers averaging 9.2 citations sit inside a portfolio of 929 total works, they contribute 1,012 citations to the numerator while consuming 12% of the denominator. Meanwhile, a single Annals of Neurology review — there are 4 of them — might generate 100+ citations on its own.

The dynamic compounds with Alzheimer's disease research, where Wiley holds 62 of 73 market works (also ~85% share). Together, these two topic clusters — essentially, Wiley's Alzheimer's & Dementia journal and related sources — add a massive volume of denominator with modest citation return.

The Dilution Formula

110 dementia papers at 9.2 average add 1,012 citations to Wiley's numerator but 110 papers to its denominator. If those same 110 papers instead averaged 25 citations (comparable to the Advanced Neuroimaging market average), Wiley's overall portfolio average would rise from 17.0 to approximately 19.7. The gap between 9.2 and 25 is the editorial opportunity — not in topic, but in quality selection, article format mix, and review commissioning within Alzheimer's and dementia research.

Hidden Stars

The Core Portfolio That Wiley Needs to Lead With

Strip away the dementia volume, and what remains is a genuinely strong neuroscience portfolio. Wiley's flagship neurology journals produce work that competes with anyone. The challenge is visibility — both externally, in how the portfolio is perceived, and internally, in how editorial resource is allocated across journals.

Two patterns stand out. First, Wiley's materials science journals — Advanced Functional Materials (28.8 avg), Angewandte Chemie International Edition (46.3 avg), Small (19.2 avg, 12 works) — are showing up in neuroscience data not by accident, but because the field of neural engineering and brain-material interfaces is growing rapidly. These journals represent Wiley's natural position in a high-growth topic that no one else in this publisher set is covering with comparable depth.

Second, the review premium in Wiley's core neuroscience journals is substantial and consistent. Across the full portfolio, reviews average 63.1 citations versus 9.4 for articles. But only 14% of Wiley's neuroscience output is reviews — the same share as Elsevier, well below Springer's 45%. Epilepsia (4 reviews, 43.5 avg) and Annals of Neurology (4 reviews, 57.6 avg) demonstrate what Wiley's review capability looks like when deployed.

Topic Analysis

Dominant Where Returns Are Low, Thin Where They Are High

The topic map for Wiley mirrors the journal map: high market share in the low-yield topics, underweight presence in the high-citation clusters. This is not a strategic choice — it is the structural consequence of allowing the Alzheimer's dementia volume to define the portfolio without a corresponding review-led, high-selectivity response in the same topic.

Strategic Recommendations

Three Moves to Unlock the Portfolio That Is Already There

  • 1
    Shift Alzheimer's & Dementia from volume publishing to high-selectivity, review-led debate publishing

    The 85% market share in dementia research is not a problem — it is an asset being operated incorrectly. The 16-citation Alzheimer's & Dementia piece on amyloid therapy regulation, the highest-cited 2025 work in that journal, shows what is possible: a critical, high-controversy perspective that the entire field has to respond to. Commission 6–8 similar pieces annually: consensus statements, clinical guidelines, debate articles, regulatory-position reviews on aducanumab/lecanemab class therapies, and deep-dive mechanistic reviews on tau vs. amyloid debate. These articles will get cited. The routine research article in this topic will not. Selectivity, not volume, is the upgrade path.

  • 2
    Formally build a "Neural Engineering" editorial franchise around Advanced Functional Materials, Angewandte Chemie International Edition, and Advanced Science

    Wiley is the only publisher in this dataset with a materials science portfolio that is actively producing neuroscience-relevant work: conducting polymer electrodes (Advanced Functional Materials), fluorescent molecular probes, brain-computer interface materials, drug-delivery nanostructures. The average citation rate for these journals in this dataset is 28–46 citations per work. A formal editorial initiative — a named "Neural Engineering" commissioned article type, cross-promoted between the three journals — would concentrate this dispersed signal into a coherent high-impact product. This is a unique Wiley asset. No competitor can replicate it without acquiring chemistry journals.

  • 3
    Double review commissioning in Annals of Neurology, Epilepsia, and Journal of Comparative Neurology

    These three journals are performing at 38–58 citations per work — competitive with PLOS and approaching Springer levels. All three have review rates of 10–40%, below Springer's 45% benchmark. Wiley reviews overall average 63.1 citations, more than 6× the article average of 9.4. A systematic commissioning program for reviews in these journals — targeting topics where the journals have institutional authority (epilepsy genetics and precision medicine, comparative neuroanatomy of disease models, neurological disease mechanisms) — would shift the weight of the high-performing journal cohort upward and become visible in the portfolio average within two JIF cycles.

The Wiley Thesis

Wiley is not a low-impact neuroscience publisher. It is a high-impact publisher with a volume problem. The stars — Annals of Neurology, British Journal of Pharmacology, Epilepsia, the materials science franchise — are buried in aggregate statistics by 172 dementia papers averaging 9.2 citations each. The path forward has two tracks running simultaneously: raise the floor by changing the article mix in the Alzheimer's/dementia domain, and raise the ceiling by investing more editorial capacity in the journals that are already performing. Both tracks are available. Neither requires a single new journal acquisition.
