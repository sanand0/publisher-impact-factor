Untapped premium

The story of PLOS in neuroscience starts with a remarkable position: second in citation density among all five publishers, at 34.3 average citations per work, despite being entirely open access and publishing far more papers than Springer Nature. The OA citation advantage is real and measurable — studies consistently show 25–50% citation lift for OA papers versus equivalent paywalled content.

But embedded in that strong average is a quiet tension. PLOS publishes 512 neuroscience works. Only 11 of them are reviews. That is a 2.1% review rate — the lowest of any publisher in this dataset, and roughly one-sixth of Springer Nature's 45%.

The Routing Problem

PLOS ONE Is Swallowing the Portfolio

Look at where PLOS's 512 neuroscience works actually land. 289 of them — 56% — are in PLOS ONE. PLOS ONE is a generalist journal, accepting all methodologically sound science. It is, by design, the journal of last resort rather than the journal of best fit. Its average citation count in this dataset: 26.8 per work.

Meanwhile, PLOS Computational Biology — a specialized journal with a coherent readership — produces 165 works averaging 41.9 citations. PLOS Biology, even more selective, manages 51 works at 56.1 citations per work. That is twice the PLOS ONE average, in a journal a quarter the size.

This routing problem is not unique to PLOS, but it is unusually legible here because the portfolio is otherwise so well-defined. The computational and systems neuroscience papers that land in PLOS ONE would perform better — for both authors and the PLOS Impact portfolio — in PLOS Computational Biology. The question is not whether the papers are good; PLOS ONE only accepts methodologically sound work. The question is whether they are being channeled to maximize their citation potential.

The 2025 data makes this concrete. PLOS Computational Biology's best 2025 papers — "A comprehensive investigation of intracortical and corticothalamic models of the alpha rhythm" (9 citations), "Meta-Reinforcement Learning reconciles surprise, value, and control in the anterior cingulate cortex" (8 citations) — already outpace the PLOS ONE 2025 average of 1.25 citations by 6–7×.

The Routing Calculus

If PLOS redirected 30 computational neuroscience papers from PLOS ONE to PLOS Computational Biology, and those papers averaged 41.9 citations instead of 26.8, the gain is 457 citations — equivalent to adding 13 additional Springer Nature-level reviews. The papers already exist; the question is which journal receives them.

The Untapped Lever

Eleven Reviews. That Is the Number.

Among 512 neuroscience papers, PLOS published exactly eleven reviews. For comparison: Wiley publishes 14% reviews, Elsevier 14%, OUP 8%, Springer 45%. PLOS is at 2.1%.

The citation premium on PLOS reviews is real: 48.6 average versus 34.0 for articles. That is a 43% uplift. Modest compared to Springer's 6× premium, but remember: PLOS articles already perform well because of the OA advantage. PLOS reviews on top of OA should be a powerful combination.

The question no one seems to have formally asked is: why does a publisher committed to open science — and with the infrastructure to commission and publish any article type — produce so few reviews?

Topic Analysis

The Computational Stronghold — and the Clinical Desert

PLOS dominates computational and systems neuroscience. It holds 61% of all Neural Dynamics and Brain Function works across the five-publisher dataset, 55% of Advanced Memory and Neural Computing, and 45% of Neural and Behavioral Psychology Studies. This is not a marginal presence — PLOS is the defining publisher for a significant slice of neuroscience.

The flip side is equally stark. Clinical and translational neuroscience is nearly absent from the PLOS portfolio. Dementia and Cognitive Impairment: 4 works of 130 in the market (3%). Alzheimer's disease: 1 of 73 (1%). Autism Spectrum Disorder: 0 of 22. Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Disorders: 1 of 25.

The missing clinical topics represent a strategic question, not just a gap. The average citation rate for Autism Spectrum Disorder works across all publishers is 156.9 — the highest of any topic in the dataset. Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Disorders averages 131.7. PLOS has essentially no presence in either.

This is partly explicable: PLOS's DNA is computational, methods-forward biology. But PLOS Medicine and PLOS Genetics exist precisely to cover translational and clinical genetics territory. Using those journals as deliberate neuroscience vehicles — attracting the right authors and commissioning reviews — would extend PLOS's impact footprint into the highest-citation territory in the field.

Strategic Recommendations

Three Moves to Close the Review Gap and Open New Territory

  • 1
    Commission 10–15 invited reviews across PLOS Computational Biology, PLoS Biology, and PLOS Digital Health — starting immediately

    The review deficit (2% vs. the market standard of 8–45%) is the single largest untapped lever in the PLOS portfolio. Target computational psychiatry, brain-computer interfaces, functional connectivity, and VR/neurotech. The 2025 PLOS Digital Health paper "Community perspectives regarding brain-computer interfaces" already achieved 7 citations in weeks — proof that the audience is there. A commissioned review in that topic would likely achieve 30–50 citations over two years, the equivalent of 1–2 additional PLoS Biology research articles.

  • 2
    Route computational neuroscience submissions from PLOS ONE to PLOS Computational Biology systematically

    Build a routing protocol for computational, systems, and imaging neuroscience submissions: if a paper lands in PLOS ONE but fits the PLOS Computational Biology scope, the handling editor should actively suggest transfer. The citation gap between the two journals (26.8 vs. 41.9 average) is a 56% improvement for the same editorial effort. This is not about rejection — PLOS's model accepts sound science regardless. It is about optimizing where sound science is published within the PLOS ecosystem.

  • 3
    Use PLOS Genetics and PLOS Medicine as deliberate vehicles for high-citation neuroscience clinical topics

    Autism Spectrum Disorder (156.9 market average citations), Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Disorders (131.7), and Mental Health (53.6) are the three highest-citation topic clusters where PLOS is essentially absent. PLOS Genetics is a natural fit for neurodevelopmental genetics; PLOS Medicine for mental health and psychiatric epidemiology. Publish a commissioned editorial or commentary in each targeting neuroscience researchers, and follow with a call for review submissions. The OA advantage means these reviews will reach more researchers than competitor equivalents behind paywalls.

The PLOS Paradox — Resolved

PLOS is the most structurally advantaged publisher in this dataset: 100% open access, strong citation density, dominant market share in the fastest-growing computational neuroscience topics. The paradox is that its impact floor is being set by a journal — PLOS ONE — designed to be broad rather than specialized, and its impact ceiling is being capped by an absence of reviews. Both constraints are reversible editorial policy choices, not structural limits. The OA advantage compounds every other improvement: more visible reviews in better-fit journals, in high-citation topics, with OA reach. That combination is uniquely available to PLOS, and it is not fully deployed.
