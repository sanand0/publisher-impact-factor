OUP Publishes the Best Neuroscience Journal Nobody Is Benchmarking Against

Brain, the flagship OUP neuroscience journal, averages 51.3 citations per work — higher than Springer Nature's portfolio average. But OUP's overall neuroscience citation rate sits at 17.5. Between those two numbers lies one of the most instructive stories in academic publishing: how a single editorial decision can quietly halve a publisher's apparent performance.

The Proceedings Problem

69 Works. 0.3 Average Citations. This Is Not a Journal Problem.

Neuro-Oncology is the largest single source in OUP's neuroscience dataset: 69 works. It is a respected, high-impact clinical journal in its own right, with a Journal Impact Factor that regularly reaches the top tier of oncology. So why does it appear in this dataset averaging 0.3 citations per work?

The answer is in the titles. Pulling the actual paper titles from the 2025 Neuro-Oncology works in this dataset reveals a consistent pattern:

Neuro-Oncology 2025 works: conference abstract formatting
Sample titles from the dataset — consistent with Society for Neuro-Oncology annual meeting abstracts
"CNSC-66. Spatially constrained gliomagenesis…"
"NCOG-06. Multimodal management of meningioma…"
"IMG-98. Metabolic response using PET RANO 1.0 criteria…"
"CAN A DEDICATED NEURO-ONCOLOGY AHP SERVICE REDUCE LENGTH OF STAY…"

These are Society for Neuro-Oncology annual meeting abstract titles. The alphanumeric codes (CNSC-66, NCOG-06, IMG-98) are session-and-submission identifiers. Neuro-Oncology publishes its annual meeting supplement as an indexed journal issue — which means these abstracts appear in OpenAlex as journal articles.

This is the most consequential finding in the OUP dataset: the 69 "Neuro-Oncology" works that are dragging OUP's average to near-zero are not research articles. They are meeting abstracts. If they are counted as citable items in the Journal Impact Factor denominator, they create a structural denominator expansion with essentially no citation return.

"The 69 Neuro-Oncology works averaging 0.3 citations are not papers. They are conference abstracts. And if they count toward the JIF denominator, OUP has a structural calculation problem — not an editorial one."

The Denominator Audit Priority

Before any editorial strategy discussion, OUP needs to answer one operational question: are the Neuro-Oncology supplement abstracts classified as citable articles in the JIF calculation? If yes, this is an immediate item for the editorial and bibliometrics team. Journals can formally designate supplement content as "not citable" — which is standard practice for meeting abstracts. The fix is administrative, not editorial. But until it is implemented, every OUP benchmarking conversation will be distorted by 69 works averaging 0.3 citations each.

The Crown Jewel

Brain Shows What OUP Can Do at Full Power

With the proceedings noise removed, Brain emerges as one of the strongest journals in this entire dataset. Its 51.3 average citation rate exceeds Springer Nature's portfolio average and trails only individual journals like Molecular Psychiatry (103.0) and Translational Psychiatry (75.8). Brain publishes deep, definitive clinical neuroscience: neurodegeneration mechanisms, epilepsy genetics, brain recovery and plasticity, neuroinflammation.

The notable gap in Brain is its review rate. Only 12 of 60 works (20%) are reviews. OUP reviews overall average 67.7 citations — versus 13.0 for articles. Every additional commissioned review in Brain is worth five research articles in terms of expected citation contribution.

◆

Physical Therapy is the sleeper strong asset in OUP's portfolio: 15 works averaging 66.9 citations. The connection to neuroscience is through neurological rehabilitation, motor recovery, and neurorehabilitation science — a growing field with an engaged clinical-research crossover readership. Physical Therapy's current review share is only 1 in 15 (7%); commissioned reviews on neural plasticity in rehabilitation and motor recovery could become landmark citable pieces.

Topic Analysis

Concentrated in Clinical Oncology, Absent in Computational Neuroscience

OUP's topic map is heavily skewed toward the journals producing the most volume. Neuro-Oncology's proceedings load means glioma-related topics dominate by sheer count — but at near-zero citation density. Strip those out, and OUP's strongest topic positions are actually in niche but high-value areas: Advanced Electron Microscopy (OUP holds 14 of 22 market works, 64% share), Musculoskeletal Pain and Rehabilitation (43% share), and Traumatic Brain Injury (26% share from Brain's clinical output).

Strategic Recommendations

Three Moves to Fix the Measurement — and Then the Strategy

  • 1
    Audit and remediate the Neuro-Oncology supplement classification: are conference abstracts designated as non-citable items in the JIF denominator?

    This is the highest-priority item in the OUP neuroscience portfolio — because until it is resolved, every other benchmarking conversation is compromised. Neuro-Oncology's annual meeting supplements are a legitimate editorial service that supports the neuro-oncology community. They should not, however, count as citable research items in an impact factor calculation. Work with Clarivate to confirm the article-type classification. If the supplement abstracts are counted as articles, submit a request for reclassification. This administrative step alone would lift OUP's reported citation density by approximately 70%.

  • 2
    Commission 5–7 invited reviews in Brain at the intersection of high-citation topics: neuroinflammation, functional connectivity in neurological disease, and genetic neurodegeneration

    Brain's 20% review share is the most immediately actionable editorial lever available to OUP. OUP reviews average 67.7 citations; Brain's existing reviews likely outperform that average. The target topics — neuroinflammation mechanisms, genetic basis of neurodegenerative diseases, recovery of function after stroke — are areas where Brain already has editorial depth and author relationships. Five commissioned reviews would add an estimated 300–400 citations to the OUP portfolio over the JIF measurement window, equivalent to converting 25–30 additional research articles into high-citation outcomes.

  • 3
    Use OUP Genetics and Nucleic Acids Research as deliberate vehicles for neurodevelopmental genetics and single-cell transcriptomics — the two fastest-growing and highest-citation topic clusters where OUP is absent or near-absent

    Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Disorders averages 131.7 market citations and OUP has zero works. Autism Spectrum Disorder averages 156.9 and OUP has one. These are not obscure topics — they are the highest-citation clusters in the dataset. OUP Genetics (29.2 avg in existing neuroscience works) is a credible entry point. Nucleic Acids Research (35.0 avg) covers the molecular mechanisms. A commissioned editorial series — "Genetics of Brain Disorders" — targeting both journals simultaneously, with systematic outreach to neurodevelopmental genetics research groups, would establish an OUP presence in territory currently owned by Elsevier and Springer.

The OUP Thesis

OUP publishes Brain. Brain beats Springer Nature's portfolio average. The problem is not what OUP publishes — it is what gets measured alongside what OUP publishes. The strategy has two stages: first, clean up the denominator (proceedings classification, off-target journals); second, build on Brain's foundation by extending its review-driven clinical neuroscience model to OUP Genetics, Physical Therapy, and Nucleic Acids Research. The clinical depth is already there. The citation ceiling is much higher than 17.5 suggests.
