The Lancet Effect — Hidden Behind 86 Works Nobody Is Citing

Elsevier publishes The Lancet. The Lancet averages nearly 96 citations per neuroscience work — more than any journal in this dataset. Yet somehow, Elsevier sits fourth out of five publishers in average citation density. The math has a culprit. Finding it changes everything.

Here is the paradox at the center of Elsevier's neuroscience position. The Lancet, an Elsevier journal, averages 95.8 citations per work in this dataset. Neuroscience Letters averages 54.6. Gastroenterology — representing brain-gut axis research — averages 60.8. These are exceptional numbers. They belong in a conversation about leading neuroscience publishers, not fourth-place ones.

And yet the aggregate number — 24.3 citations per work across 584 papers — tells a different story. Something is dragging the average down. Three journals are responsible for most of it. Together, they add 124 papers to Elsevier's denominator and contribute only 272 citations — an average of 2.2 citations each.

Denominator Drag

Three Journals Hiding a Much Better Publisher

The Journal of the Neurological Sciences is Elsevier's most-published neuroscience source in this dataset: 86 works. Its average citation count: 2.9. It is also, structurally, not the journal you would design if your goal was neuroscience impact. Its scope is broad clinical neurology — case reports, short communications, conference material — not the high-signal basic or systems neuroscience that drives citation density.

Then there is CHEST Journal: 24 works, 0.8 average citations. CHEST is a respiratory medicine journal. Its appearance in Elsevier's neuroscience portfolio — likely through sleep-disordered breathing and respiratory neurophysiology — represents a definitional edge case, not a strategic neuroscience bet.

Finally, The New Scientist: 14 works, 0.3 average citations. The New Scientist is a popular science magazine. It does not produce citable research. Its inclusion in any impact-factor analysis is a data-sorting artifact.

"Remove the Journal of the Neurological Sciences, CHEST, and The New Scientist — and Elsevier's average citation rate rises from 24.3 to 30.3. Same publisher. Different story."

The Denominator Math

124 works from three drag sources contribute 272 total citations — 2.2 per work. The remaining 460 Elsevier works average 30.3 each. The drag journals are diluting a publisher that, on its core neuroscience output, is competitive with PLOS and closing in on Springer. That number — 30.3 — is the real baseline for Elsevier's neuroscience strategy.

The Review Premium

Elsevier Has the Highest Review Citation Rate. And Uses It the Least.

The most striking number in the Elsevier dataset is one that almost no one has noticed: Elsevier reviews average 122.5 citations. That is higher than any other publisher's review average. Higher than Springer Nature's 97.8. Higher than Wiley's 63.1 and OUP's 67.7. Elsevier's review engine, when engaged, is the most powerful in the field.

The problem: it is barely engaged. Reviews make up 14% of Elsevier's neuroscience output — exactly matching Wiley, and a third of Springer's 45%. The citation math makes the cost of this under-engagement concrete: each review that displaces an article in Elsevier's portfolio adds roughly 114 citations (122.5 minus 8.5). Each additional commissioned review is worth more than thirteen additional articles.

The hidden insight here is about The Lancet. With 63 works and only 3 reviews, The Lancet is the strongest journal in Elsevier's portfolio on a per-work basis — and also one of the most underutilized in review format. A single invited review in The Lancet on a high-profile neuroscience topic — functional connectivity and depression, brain biomarkers for treatment response, or the genetics of neurodevelopmental disorders — would likely become one of the most-cited works in Elsevier's entire neuroscience portfolio.

◆

Brain Research is quietly building momentum: 34 works, 15.3 avg, 11 reviews. The 2025 standout — "The neuroplastic brain: current breakthroughs and emerging frontiers," a review already at 50 citations — demonstrates what Brain Research can do when editorial selection is tight. This is the model to replicate across the portfolio.

Topic Analysis

Surprising Strength — and Critical Gaps

Elsevier's topic map contains a surprise. In the two highest-citation topic clusters in this entire dataset — Autism Spectrum Disorder (market average 156.9 citations) and Genetics and Neurodevelopmental Disorders (131.7) — Elsevier has respectively 45% and 48% market share. These are dominant positions in highly cited territory. They are not reflected in the aggregate publisher average because the high-citation papers in these topics are being averaged with JNS's 2.9 and CHEST's 0.8.

The Lancet is the bridge between Elsevier's existing strengths and its growth opportunities. A curated series of Lancet Neurology-style reviews on functional connectivity, genetics of neurodevelopmental disorders, and computational psychiatry would simultaneously reinforce Elsevier's topic leadership and generate the review-format citations that the portfolio currently lacks.

One caution: the 46% market share in "Empathy and Medical Education" looks like strength, but the topic averages only 8.1 citations across the whole market. This is a concentration in a low-yield topic — the opposite of where Elsevier's topic effort should go.

Strategic Recommendations

Three Moves to Reveal — and Grow — the Real Elsevier

  • 1
    Redefine the "Elsevier neuroscience" portfolio: exclude Journal of the Neurological Sciences, CHEST, and The New Scientist from benchmarking and focus editorial investment on the core journals

    This single change — analytical rather than operational — reveals a publisher averaging 30.3 citations per work instead of 24.3. The underlying editorial reality already exists; the framing does not. For internal strategy and client reporting, the core Elsevier neuroscience journals should be: Neuroscience Letters, Brain Research, European Neuropsychopharmacology, Heliyon, The Lancet, and Journal of Biological Chemistry. Track these separately. Then use the gap analysis to drive commissioning toward higher-yield work.

  • 2
    Commission 6–8 invited reviews in The Lancet and Neuroscience Letters on Autism/Genetics, Functional Connectivity, and Computational Psychiatry

    Elsevier's review citation rate (122.5 per review) is the highest of any publisher. The Lancet has only 3 reviews in 63 neuroscience works. The math is compelling: a single Lancet neuroscience review in Autism Spectrum Disorder — where Elsevier already has 48% market share and the topic averages 156.9 citations — could alone generate 100–200 citations over two years. That is equivalent to 12–24 additional research articles in terms of impact metric contribution. Neuroscience Letters' 24 existing reviews (avg 54.6) confirm the model works at scale.

  • 3
    Double down on European Neuropsychopharmacology's review engine — it is the underpowered equivalent of Springer's Neuropsychopharmacology franchise

    European Neuropsychopharmacology has 60 works but only 7.8 avg citations and 6 reviews (10%). Compare Springer's Neuropsychopharmacology: 50 works, 75.4 avg, 26 reviews (52%). The difference is almost entirely format mix. A commissioning program targeting invited reviews in psychopharmacology, computational psychiatry, and precision psychopharmacology — matching Springer's content strategy in a journal Elsevier already owns — could move European Neuropsychopharmacology from a midrange journal to a citation anchor for the entire portfolio.

The Elsevier Thesis

Elsevier is not a fourth-place neuroscience publisher. It is a top-three publisher being measured with its ballast included. The Lancet, Neuroscience Letters, and a dominant position in the two highest-citation topic clusters represent a genuinely strong portfolio. The path to improvement is not acquiring new journals or chasing new topics — it is removing the measurement noise, engaging the review engine already inside Neuroscience Letters and The Lancet, and letting the actual portfolio speak for itself.
